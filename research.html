Temporal dynamics of species ranges

There are multiple ways that species can experience a shift in range, whether it is from being introduced into novel environments outside of their native range or due to shifting environmental conditions. Some species are capable of rapidly expanding their ranges, while others are limited. What can we learn from existing locality data on temporal dynamics of range shifts?

Interactions between plants and their animal dispersers

Many plants rely on a vast suite of frugivorous animals to disperse their seeds. Yet not all frugivorous species are equal in terms of the quality of dispersal services they offer to plants. In addition, the composition of the community of potential animal dispersers available to disperse a plant's seeds may vary dramatically both throughout the dispersal season and from one year to the next. How do plants cope with temporal variability in dispersal?

Invasive species

Invasive species are recognized as one of the second greatest threats facing biodiversity after habitat loss. But not all introduced species become problematic invaders. There are many challenges associated with surviving and reproducing in a novel environment. In spreading to a new environment, some species may leave behind their native enemies and are thus able to devote more of their resources towards survival and reproduction instead of defense against herbivory (the enemy-release hypothesis), but they also may have left behind their mutualists and may encounter novel interaction partners in their new environment.

Integral projection models

When modeling the dynamics of a population, we must think carefully about the underlying demographic processes that govern the population growth rate - survival, growth and reproduction. How do differences amongst individuals contribute to the probability that an individual will survive to time t, grow to be a certain size by time t, or reproduce and produce a certain number of offspring by time t? In many plants, current size (a continuous state variable) is a good predictor for all of these probabilities.
